SYGN 550 — Intelligent Geosystems

An interdisciplinary graduate course for students in engineering, geoscience and computer science at Mines.

#content()

Summary

This graduate course was created and is taught with my colleagues in SmartGeo. Our students have diverse backgrounds, with degrees in computer science, environmental science, civil engineering, geology, or geophysics. This course provides a common technical foundation, to help them work in two or more of these disciplines to create intelligent geosystems that can sense and adapt to their environments.

Following an introduction and overview, the course consists primarily of four modules covering sensing, data processing, modeling, and decision support. The three-week data processing module that I lead is a hands-on - learn by doing - survey of

  • Basic digital signal processing
  • Wiener and time-varying filters
  • Interpolation of scattered data

As we analyze, implement and apply various processing methods to previously recorded data, we will highlight those methods that might best be implemented in real-time in embedded sensors.

Evaluation

Students will work in teams to complete assignments. Grades for this module are based on your contributions to the data processing that (1) we all perform in class and (2) your team performs outside of class.

While some students in each team will naturally be quicker to implement various processing methods, all members of each team must understand those methods and be able to describe their implementations to others.

Resources

Although I will use freely available open-source software in class, students may use any computer programming languages and development environments in which they are most productive. Students must have a valid license for any commercial software used.

Information on data processing, including complete implementations of various algorithms, is readily available on the web. Indeed, you can learn much of the material in this module by studying freely available information that you find there. I encourage you to use such information, just as you would in your own research, while acknowledging your sources, and understanding implementations and limitations in everything that you use.

Text (for reference only)

Discrete-Time Signal Processing, 2nd or 3rd Edition, by Oppenheim and Schafer. Purchase of this text is not required for this module. However, if I could own only one book on digital signal processing, this would be the one.

The most recent 3rd edition is now available. Used copies of the 2nd edition are also available, and two copies of it are on reserve in Arthur Lakes Library.
